Glossary & Methodology

What the numbers mean and how they're ranked.

Everything is a percentile

Each stat is shown as a percentile from 0 to 100 — the share of peers a player beats. 90 means better than 90% of comparable players that season. Color runs cold (low) to elite (high); for stats where less is better, the percentile is flipped so high is always good.

Ranked within position

Percentiles are pooled by position: forwards vs. forwards, defensemen vs. defensemen, goalies vs. goalies, recomputed each season. A defenseman at the 90th percentile in offense is elite among defensemen, not against forwards.

All vs. 5-on-5

All situations include power play and penalty kill; 5-on-5 isolates even-strength play, stripping out special-teams role. Selke (defensive forward) is judged on 5-on-5 so penalty-killers aren't punished for the chances the PK concedes.

OVR & the awards

A player's OVR blends their group percentiles with the same weights their trophy uses — Hart for forwards (offense-led), Norris for defensemen, Vezina for goalies — then that composite is re-ranked into its own percentile.

Who qualifies

Skaters need more than 200 minutes or 20 games; goalies need more than 15 games or 600 minutes. Awards also fold in a workload factor so a hot small sample can't out-rank a full season.

A caveat on defense

The on-ice “against” stats (xGoals Against/60) reward sheltered usage — a bottom-six player who faces weak competition can post strong suppression. It's the best team-defense signal here, not a pure individual-skill measure.

Reading the Leaderboard

Every number is a percentile (0–100) ranked within the chosen pool — forwards vs. forwards, defensemen vs. defensemen, goalies vs. goalies — so blue is cold and red is elite. OVR is itself a percentile: each player's group scores are blended with the same weights their trophy uses (Hart for forwards — offense-led, so sheltered defensive minutes don't dominate; Norris for defensemen; Vezina for goalies). That skill composite is then mixed 75/25 with workload — per-game ice time (min/GP) for skaters, total ice time for goalies — and re-ranked across the pool. Group columns are bold; the rest are the individual stats behind them.

Grey goalie cells (Shot Quality Faced, Shots Faced/60) are workload context, neither good nor bad. Some stats are better when low (xGoals Against, Giveaways, Rebounds/Shot); their percentile is already flipped so high is always good.

How the Awards Are Picked

Each trophy is scored from the season's percentile profiles — the same Offense / Two-Way / Defense (and goalie) group scores shown on a player card — combined with simple, fixed weights, then blended 75/25 with a workload factor — per-game ice time (min/GP) for skaters, total ice time for goalies — so a lightly-used player can't out-rank a true workhorse. They are the model's view, not the real voters'.

Rookies (Calder) are approximated as players in their first qualifying season within this dataset (2008–09 onward), so it is unavailable for 2008–09 and can misflag late-arriving veterans.

Cross-position & the MVP. Most numbers on a player card are ranked within position, so a defenseman at the 99th percentile in offense is elite among defensemen, not vs. forwards. The Hart is the exception: it scores the whole league — forwards, defensemen and goalies — each on their own role, with skaters compared on one all-skater scale. On that honest footing forwards usually out-value defensemen, so D rarely crack the top five, and a dominant goalie can win outright.

Goalie shutouts and win–loss records are not in the MoneyPuck shot data, so the summary shows GAA, save percentage and games played.
